VideoA11yGPT: Method and Datasets for Accessible Video Description

1Arizona State University, 2University of Rochester
Dataset Paper Code

Abstract

Video description is essential for blind and low vision (BLV) users to access visual content. Yet, existing video description datasets are often limited by the quality of human annotation, resulting in video accessibility not meeting the needs of BLV users. To bridge this gap, we introduce VideoA11yGPT, an approach that leverages state-of-the-art multimodal large language models (MLLMs) to generate video descriptions for BLV individuals. Specifically, we curated 42 accessibility guidelines for video descriptions and prompted MLLMs to generate or revise descriptions for video datasets. Our rigorous experiments across 15 video categories with sighted and BLV participants demonstrated that the descriptions generated by VideoA11yGPT surpass the original human annotations in terms of clarity, accuracy, objectivity, descriptiveness, and overall user satisfaction. We present two new video description datasets, namely, YouCook2-BLV and VALOR32K-BLV tailored for BLV individuals and demonstrate that VideoA11yGPT can provide high-quality descriptions for any video content.

Approach Overview

VideoA11yGPT focuses on generating accurate descriptions free from grammatical errors and suitable for BLV people based on video frames and, when available, existing human descriptions. First, keyframes are extracted from the input video. Then, prompts, AD guidelines, keyframes, and optional human annotations are provided to GPT-4V, which subsequently generates accessible video descriptions

We gathered a total of 154 audio description (AD) guidelines from various online sources, including Netflix Accessibility Guidelines, Ofcom Guidelines, Media Access Canada Guidelines, and the Described and Captioned Media Program. These guidelines are intended for professional and volunteer describers working on video content ranging from entertainment to education. After collating these guidelines, we removed duplicates and categorized them, focusing on whether they could be effectively used to prompt a vision language model. This categorization process excluded guidelines that required subjective human judgment or depended on audio cues, leading to a set of 42 optimized AD guidelines.

Experiments

Study 1

We assessed the video descriptions generated by VideoA11yGPT using human subject studies that involved both sighted and BLV individuals. We created an evaluation dataset of public videos and generated descriptions for each using VideoA11yGPT. We defined four metrics to measure description quality and conducted two studies with sighted participants on Amazon Mechanical Turk and one online study with BLV participants. All studies were approved by the Institutional Review Board (IRB), and participants gave informed consent by proceeding with the online surveys.

We conducted an Amazon MTurk experiment with sighted users to evaluate the quality differences in video descriptions generated by open-source and proprietary machine learning language models (MLLMs) using our accessibility guidelines (AD guidelines). The models tested were Video-LLaVA as the open-source MLLM and GPT-4V as the proprietary MLLM.

  1. Video-LLaVA + AD Guidelines: Used our AD guidelines and video keyframes as input, with descriptions generated by Video-LLaVA.
  2. Video-LLaVA + AD Guidelines + Human Annotation: Similar to the first condition, but also incorporated human annotations.
  3. GPT-4V + AD Guidelines: Used our AD guidelines and video keyframes as input, with descriptions generated by GPT-4V.
  4. GPT-4V + AD Guidelines + Human Annotation: Similar to the third condition, but also included human annotations.

We recruited 150 MTurk participants, and each participant watched one long video and one short video clip, and read four descriptions for each video. Participants rated each description based on a scale from "extremely bad" to "extremely good" across four quality metrics. Below are the results from this study.

We used the related-samples Friedman Test to analyze our data, as the user ratings are ordinal. The test revealed a significant effect of the description method. When analyzing both long videos and video clips together, pairwise comparisons indicated that VideoA11yGPT and VideoA11yGPT without human annotations significantly outperformed both versions of VideoA11yLLaVA across all four metrics, with p-values less than 0.05. Given these results, we focused on GPT-4V as the machine learning language model in subsequent studies.

Study 2

In the second study, we recruited 150 new MTurk participants to evaluate the quality of descriptions from VideoA11yGPT compared to those generated by human annotation alone or by GPT-4V alone. Each participant viewed one long video and one short video clip, rating five different descriptions for each video:

  1. Human Annotation: Included descriptions generated by humans.
  2. GPT-4V: Utilized only the keyframes extracted from the videos. The prompt did not include our AD guidelines or original human annotations.
  3. GPT-4V + Human Annotation: Combined human annotations with keyframes to generate descriptions.
  4. GPT-4V + AD Guidelines (VideoA11yGPT without HA): Used our AD guidelines and video keyframes as inputs for GPT-4V to generate descriptions.
  5. GPT-4V + AD Guidelines + Human Annotation (VideoA11yGPT): Similar to the fourth description but also included human annotations.

The study results indicated that VideoA11yGPT outperformed other methods across all metrics, with statistically significant improvements (p-values under 0.002). VideoA11yGPT without human annotations also showed significant enhancements over Human Annotation, GPT-4V, and GPT-4V + Human Annotation in three out of four metrics except for accuracy, where all p-values were below 0.05. This suggests the effectiveness of our approach in enhancing video description quality.

Study 3

In the final study, we recruited 17 blind or low vision (BLV) participants, aged 21 to 44 years, to evaluate the effectiveness of VideoA11yGPT. The group consisted of 13 males, 3 females, and 1 who preferred not to specify their gender, all from the United States. Among them, 7 were completely blind, and 10 were legally blind, with visual acuities ranging from 20/500 to 20/1000. We selected five videos from various categories for evaluation: Entertainment, How-to and Instructional, Sports, Pets and Animals, and People and Vlogs. The videos featured both human annotations and descriptions generated by VideoA11yGPT as audio descriptions, placed before the video segments they described, following AD best practices.

Each BLV participant watched all five videos twice, with the two types of audio descriptions presented in random order. They rated each description on a 10-point scale, from 1 (extremely bad) to 10 (extremely good), to capture more nuanced feedback. Participants also chose which video description they preferred and explained their reasons.

The results from the BLV participants indicated that VideoA11yGPT descriptions consistently outperformed human annotations across all video categories and metrics. Specifically, VideoA11yGPT was chosen over human annotations with a selection rate of 91.76% (78 out of 85 instances), demonstrating significant enhancement in how BLV individuals understand and enjoy video content.

Participants highlighted several reasons for preferring VideoA11yGPT, including the detailed content, clarity, and accuracy of the descriptions, which depicted precise actions and settings effectively. They also appreciated the narrative quality, essential background, and contextual details provided, along with the effective synchronization of descriptions with the unfolding events and the comprehensive coverage of aspects often overlooked in human annotations. These factors collectively improved the viewing experience for BLV users.

Datasets

Based on the positive outcomes, we used VideoA11yGPT (GPT-4V + AD Guidelines + Human Annotation) to generate high-quality video descriptions for two well-known datasets in the computer vision community: VALOR32K and YouCook2. This application is in line with the MIT license permissions associated with these datasets, which allow free use, modification, and distribution.

We have developed two new accessible datasets using our method: VALOR32K-BLV and YouCook2-BLV. These datasets aim to enhance accessibility for the BLV community by providing tailored video descriptions that improve understanding and engagement with video content.

VALOR32K-BLV

The VALOR32K-BLV dataset has 29,635 video clips (23,268 trainset, 3,179 valset, and 3,188 testset) across 15 categories, each lasting 10 seconds. We generated these categories alongside the video descriptions using GPT-4V, based on the 15 categories in Section~\ref{sec:videos}. To verify categorization accuracy, we randomly sampled 5 videos from each category (75 videos in total) and recruited 225 MTurk participants (152 males, 71 females, aged 21--68 years) to rate the correctness of the assigned categories. A video was deemed misclassified if at least two out of three votes indicated an incorrect category. Our results confirmed that 96\% (72 out of 75) of the videos were accurately categorized.

The average caption length in the VALOR32K-BLV dataset is 48.92 words, which is notably longer than 19.68 words in the VALOR32K dataset. Figure 6 presents the caption length distribution in VALOR32K-BLV, with the majority of captions ranging between 40-60 words (35.4%) and 20-40 words (33.8%). Captions shorter than 20 words or longer than 80 words comprise only 3.9% and 8.8%, respectively. The figure also shows video distribution across 15 categories; How-to and Instructional (18.8%), Music (16.9%), and Film and Animation (15%) dominate, while News and Politics and Nonprofits and Activism each make up less than 1% of the videos.

YouCook2-BLV

The YouCook2-BLV dataset features 1,600 cooking videos (1,190 trainset, 410 valset) with an average of 5.27 minutes in length. The average caption length in the YouCook2-BLV is 104.81 words which is considerably longer than 67.55 words in YouCook2. The distribution of caption lengths in YouCook2-BLV (Figure 7) has a wide range, with most captions falling within the 100-120 word range (28.6% of the total). This is followed by captions spanning 80-100 words (25.5%), fewer than 80 words (18.8%), and those ranging from 120-140 words (15.8%). Longer captions, between 140-160 words and over 160 words, comprise 6.8% and 4.6% of the data, respectively.

The caption length distribution in YouCook2-BLV shows a wide range, with the majority of captions falling within the 100-120 word range, accounting for 28.6% of the total. This is followed by captions that contain 80-100 words (25.5%), and those within the 120-140 word range (18.8%). Captions shorter than 80 words and those longer than 160 words make up 6.8% and 4.6% of the dataset, respectively. This broader and longer range of caption lengths reflects an emphasis on providing more comprehensive descriptions to enhance accessibility for BLV viewers.
